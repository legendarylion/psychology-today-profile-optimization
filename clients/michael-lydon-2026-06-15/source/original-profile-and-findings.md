Key Findings
1. Your opening line buries your strongest hook
Psychology Today shows only roughly the first 150 characters of your personal statement in directory search results — that snippet determines whether a seeker clicks your card or the next therapist's. Right now it opens with the smoke alarm question, which is a great metaphor once someone is reading, but seekers scanning 20 profiles are pattern-matching for their problem in plain language. The rewrite leads with symptoms first, then deploys the metaphor. It's a re-sequencing, not a rewrite — your voice stays fully intact.
2. Your Top Specialties don't match your differentiation
Anxiety, Trauma and PTSD, and Depression are the three most saturated filters on the platform. Your ideal clients — the ones navigating narcissistic wounding, spiritual awakening, and crisis of faith — filter by Spirituality, where you'd be one of very few credible options in Massachusetts rather than one of hundreds. Recommendation: keep Anxiety and Trauma and PTSD as volume anchors, and swap Depression for Spirituality. Depression stays in your Expertise list and your written copy, so those seekers still find you.
3. Conceptual language needed balancing with practical symptom/outcome language
Your depth is a major asset, but first-time therapy seekers move quickly and search in plain terms. In the rewrites that follow, phrases like “patterns from early emotional learning” become “patterns rooted in childhood emotional experiences, attachment wounds, and chronic self-doubt,” and “cultivate safety and well-being with Realization Process practices” becomes “help your nervous system feel safer, calmer, and more grounded in daily life.” Same meaning, same voice — just immediately graspable by someone newer to this work.
4. Your consultation length is inconsistent — pick one
Your bio says free 30-minute consult; the profile's call-to-action blocks say 15 minutes. It's a small thing, but inconsistencies like this quietly erode trust at the exact moment someone is deciding to reach out. My recommendation is to standardize on 30 minutes: at your fee level, a longer consult is a genuine differentiator, and clients seeking depth-oriented work tend to need more than 15 minutes to feel a fit. The trade-off is your time — if your consult-to-client conversion rate is high, 30 minutes is easily worth it; if you field many unqualified inquiries, 15 protects your calendar. The new copy in this document assumes 30. If you choose 15 instead, simply change “free 30-minute consultation” to “free 15-minute consultation” in Part 3 of the personal statement — then make sure the same number appears everywhere on the profile.
5. One credential display item to verify
Your Qualifications section currently displays “Prelicense / LCSW223441, Supervised by Margaret Fasolo” alongside “9 years in practice” and the LCSW headline. This may be exactly how Massachusetts licensure displays through Psychology Today's verification — in MA, the LCSW is a supervised-practice license, distinct from the independent-practice LICSW — but a careful seeker who scrolls down could find the combination confusing or wonder about the mismatch. Two things to do here: first, check your Psychology Today member dashboard to confirm the verification display reflects your current licensure status (and contact PT support if it's outdated). Second, if the display is accurate, consider adding a clarifying sentence to your Qualifications blurb so seekers understand it before they wonder. A template you can adapt to your actual circumstances:
I hold an LCSW in Massachusetts and practice under the clinical supervision of Margaret Fasolo, LICSW, as part of the state's licensure pathway. My nine years in practice include both clinical work and transformational healing practice as a certified Realization Process and Coherence Therapy practitioner.
Important: edit this so every claim is accurate to your situation before publishing — only you know the precise details of your license status and practice history.
6. Your textbook credential is underleveraged
Your contributor chapter in Unlocking the Emotional Brain is your single strongest trust signal, and it currently sits in your third paragraph. The rewrites move it into more prominent positions in both your personal statement and your Qualifications section, and I'd also recommend mentioning it verbally in your profile video.
Your New Personal Statement
The three parts below map to Psychology Today's three personal statement fields. Copy in the shaded blocks is ready to paste.
Part 1 — Ideal Client
Despite years of inner work, do the same cycles keep returning — anxiety, self-doubt, people-pleasing, low self-worth, or the fear of being “too much” or “not enough”? These patterns aren't random. They're rooted in early emotional learning, attachment wounds, and trauma your nervous system still carries. Most therapy quiets the “smoke alarm” of your symptoms. My work resolves the actual “fire” underneath — often in as few as 12–24 sessions, without endless analysis or years of reliving your past. If you're ready to live as your true self, free from guilt, shame, and staying small, I'd be honored to work with you.
Why this works: the search snippet now reads “Despite years of inner work, do the same cycles keep returning — anxiety, self-doubt, people-pleasing, low self-worth...” — symptom keywords right where seekers scan.
Part 2 — Approach
When you resolve the fire, the smoke alarm quiets on its own. I blend Coherence Therapy and Realization Process — two gentle, experiential methods that uncover and dissolve the unconscious root causes of anxiety, depression, trauma, and chronic self-doubt. Many of my clients are spiritually oriented adults navigating emotional pain alongside spiritual awakening, “dark night of the soul” experiences, or a crisis of faith. This isn't coping skills or symptom management; when the root cause resolves, lasting change feels natural and effortless.
Part 3 — Authority + Call to Action
My work is featured in the therapy textbook Unlocking the Emotional Brain (2nd Ed.), where Chapter 21 details how I helped a client resolve complex attachment trauma. I see clients online throughout Massachusetts, with 60- or 90-minute sessions available. Schedule a free 30-minute consultation to explore what deep resolution of your current issue could look like.
